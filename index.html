For the Country That Never Loved Him Back

In Memory of Leonard Matlovich

They etched his name on a grave without rights,

"When I was in the military, they gave me a medal for killing two men and a discharge for loving one."

In silence, he served a nation that feared his truth,

Wearing boots heavy with the weight of a double life.

Each salute became a whisper,

A hope that maybe love and duty could share a uniform.

But the flag that draped his coffin did not know his name,

Only the shame it assigned him for who he loved.

He stood—open, unashamed—

And was buried by the same country that asked for silence again.

Today, we unearth him with every truth we dare to speak,

In TikToks, in protests, in drag shows defiant and divine.

He walks with us—

In ballrooms turned battlegrounds,

In love letters mailed from barracks, unopened, returned.

This country may never have loved him back,

But we will. Over and over again,

With every queer breath, every uncloseted morning,

We will remember Leonard, not just in mourning—

But in the living.
