"For the Country That Never Loved Him Back"
They gave him a folded flag,
not for who he kissed—
but for how many he killed.
He may have kissed just one,
but it was the wrong war.
They say heroes die once.
But some men—
some men die twice.
Once for their country...
and once when it spits them out
like a tooth knocked loose
in a bar fight called history.
Leonard Matlovich.
Not a martyr.
Not a mistake.
A man.
Who wore his heart like a grenade—
pulled the pin,
and waited to see
if America would flinch.
They stitched his wounds,
but not their shame.
They pinned medals over silence,
discharged him
for a kiss that dared to exist
in daylight.
He didn't die in Vietnam.
He died in the denial
that his love
was worth less than his trigger finger.
And still—
he stood.
Back straight,
voice steady,
with the truth strapped across his chest
like a sash of defiance.
He may have walked among the ghosts
with a silence he'd sharpened into armor.
But he wasn't quiet.
No, Leonard never marched alone.
Not really.
Because every queer kid
staring at that stone
wondering if it's worth it—
whether to serve, to forgive,
to hope—
is walking with him.
Footnote to the Universe:
Let no nation expect me
to die for its dreams
until it proves it won't kill mine.
Give me a country
that tattoos freedom onto the inside of its ribcage
before it asks for blood.
—Mokhosi Katse
